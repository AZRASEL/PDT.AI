About Photodynamic Therapy

Understanding the science and benefits of PDT

What is PDT? History Importance

1. What is Photodynamic Therapy (PDT)?

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) is a medical treatment that uses a combination of a light‑sensitive drug and a specific color of light to destroy unwanted or diseased cells.

The drug, known as a photosensitizer, is given as a cream, pill, or injection and then allowed to accumulate in the target tissue more than in normal tissue.

When activated, the photosensitizer transfers energy to oxygen naturally present in the tissue, creating reactive oxygen species that damage cell structures and blood vessels.

Because both the drug and light must be present in the same place at the same time, PDT can offer selective destruction of diseased tissue while sparing surrounding healthy tissue.

2. A Brief History of Photodynamic Therapy

The roots of PDT go back over a century to early observations that certain dyes could make microorganisms highly sensitive to light.

Around 1900, researchers described the “photodynamic action” of dyes like acridine, laying the conceptual foundation for PDT.

In the mid‑20th century, researchers developed compounds that preferentially accumulated in tumors, bringing PDT into clinical oncology research at Roswell Park.

This work led to first‑generation clinical drugs and the formal recognition of PDT as a treatment for esophageal, lung, and skin cancers.

The field has since expanded with new photosensitizers, improved light delivery systems, and applications beyond cancer into dermatology and ophthalmology.

3. Why Photodynamic Therapy is Important

PDT offers a targeted, minimally invasive treatment for cancers and precancers, preserving organ function and causing less scarring than traditional methods.

Its selectivity arises from controllable components—drug, light, and oxygen—making it especially valuable for localized tumors.

Compared with surgery or radiation, PDT often causes less scarring and preserves organ function, and can be repeated if necessary.

Beyond direct tumor killing, PDT can also shut down tumor blood vessels and stimulate local immune responses.

As technologies improve, PDT is increasingly seen as an important part of the broader precision‑medicine toolbox for oncology and other fields.
