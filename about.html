About Photodynamic Therapy

Understanding the science and benefits of PDT

What is PDT?

Photodynamic Therapy (PDT) is an innovative medical treatment that combines three essential elements: a photosensitizer (light-sensitive drug), light of a specific wavelength, and oxygen naturally present in tissues.

PDT has been successfully used for decades to treat various cancers and precancerous conditions. For oral cancer, PDT offers unique advantages due to the accessibility of the oral cavity.

History and Development

1900s - Discovery

The phenomenon underlying PDT was discovered in Munich, Germany, when researchers observed that certain dyes combined with light could kill cells.

1970s-1990s - Clinical Trials

Extensive research led to FDA approval of Photofrin for various cancers. PDT began being used for head and neck cancers with promising results.

2000s-Present - Innovation

Development of second-generation photosensitizers, improved light delivery systems, and combination therapies have enhanced PDT effectiveness significantly.

Key Advantages of PDT

Selective Targeting

PDT targets malignant cells while sparing healthy tissues. Photosensitizers accumulate preferentially in rapidly dividing cancer cells.

Minimal Invasiveness

Unlike surgery, PDT doesn't require cutting or removal of tissue. Light is delivered directly to the affected area using fiber optic devices.

Functional Preservation

PDT preserves critical anatomical structures and functions. Patients maintain their ability to speak, swallow, and taste normally.

Reduced Side Effects

Compared to chemotherapy and radiation, PDT has fewer and less severe side effects.

Ideal Candidates for PDT

✓

Early-Stage Cancer

T1N0M0 oral squamous cell carcinoma - small, localized tumors without lymph node involvement

✓

Precancerous Lesions

Oral leukoplakia, erythroplakia, lichen planus, and verrucous hyperplasia with dysplasia

✓

Recurrent Disease

Patients with recurrent oral cancer where surgery or radiation is not feasible

✓

Adjuvant Therapy

As complementary treatment following surgery or in combination with other modalities
